Lu Xun

This article is about the Chinese writer. For the asteroid, see 23305 Lu Xun.

Lu Xun (Traditional Chinese: 魯迅; Simplified Chinese: 鲁迅; pinyin: Lǔ Xùn; Wade-Giles: Lu Hsün), also known as Zhou Shuren (pen name: Lu Xun), was a paramount Chinese writer, often hailed as the father of modern Chinese literature. 

Born: September 25, 1881, Shaoxing, Zhejiang province, China
Died: October 19, 1936, Shanghai, China

Early Life and Education
Lu Xun was born into a traditionally esteemed and wealthy family that faced a steep decline due to his grandfather's imprisonment for examination fraud. Influenced by the fall from grace and his father's subsequent illness and death, Lu Xun pursued a career in medicine before switching to literature as a means to effect social change. He studied medicine in Japan where the realization that China needed cultural and spiritual awakening more than physical healing led him to abandon his medical studies for literature.

Career and Legacy
Lu Xun's career in literature began with his influential role in the May Fourth Movement, advocating for modernization and a critique of traditional Chinese ideals. His short stories, essays, and translations marked a shift in Chinese literature towards vernacular language and socially conscious themes. Some of his well-known works include "A Madman's Diary" (1918), “The True Story of Ah Q” (1922), and the collections "Call to Arms" (1923) and "Wandering" (1925). 

In "A Madman’s Diary," Lu Xun's pioneering use of vernacular Chinese and the critique of traditional values introduced a new literary era. Meanwhile, "The True Story of Ah Q" is notable for its satirical portrayal of a delusional peasant, epitomizing a failure to comprehend one's social conditions, thereby coining the term "Ah Q spirit."

Influence
Lu Xun's contributions influenced subsequent generations and played an integral role in shaping 20th-century Chinese literature. His acerbic wit and incisive social criticism remain a significant part of Chinese education, often reflecting the dynamic interplay between traditional norms and the ushering in of modern consciousness.

Lu Xun was deeply skeptical of any form of unquestioned dogma and maintained a stance of individual thinking throughout his life. Although he never joined the Communist Party, after his death, Mao Zedong praised Lu Xun, and his works became instrumental in the development of the Chinese communist literary canon. 

Translations of his Stories
Lu Xun translated various foreign literary works into Chinese, contributing to the exchange of cultural and intellectual ideas. His efforts in translation underscored his belief in literature as a tool for social enlightenment and radical transformation. His collections, such as "Wild Grass," offer unique insights into marginalized voices within society, while his memoir "Morning Blossoms Gathered at Dusk" reflects personal narratives that connect broader social experiences.

Notable Family Members
Lu Xun was the elder brother of Zhou Zuoren, a notable Chinese essayist, and translator, who also played a key role in the cultural milieu of early 20th-century China.

Modern Impact
Lu Xun's status as a venerated figure in modern Chinese literature stems from his masterful storytelling, merging vivid characterizations with underlying messages aimed at inciting change. His works, spanning from socio-political critiques to explorations of individual psyches within feudal society, remain a testament to his influence as a moral compass in China's cultural evolution.
