Great Wall of China
Also known as Wan-li Changcheng
Category: Geography & Travel

**Overview:**
The Great Wall of China, renowned as one of the world's most extraordinary constructions, is a series of fortifications made of stone, brick, tamped earth, wood, and other materials. It generally runs east to west along the historical northern borders of China to protect the Chinese states and empires against the raids and invasions of the various nomadic groups of the Eurasian Steppe.

**History and Construction:**
The construction history of the Great Wall dates back to the 7th century BCE, with several walls being built as early as the Warring States period, which were later joined together and made stronger. The most significant construction occurred under Qin Shi Huang, the first Emperor of China, around 220 BCE. However, the majority of the existing wall is the result of a rebuilding effort by the Ming Dynasty (1368â€“1644).

**Geography:**
The Great Wall extends from the Dandong in the east to the Gobi Desert 1,500 miles (2,400 km) to the west and traverses 15 provinces. This iconic structure stretches over 13,170 miles, with the most celebrated section of the Ming Dynasty extending approximately 5,500 miles.

**Structure:**
The wall measures from 15 to 39 feet in height and up to 32 feet thick in certain regions. It features a series of watchtowers, barracks, garrison stations, and signaling capabilities through smoke or fire. The majority of the wall is constructed from bricks and stones, but some parts are made of wood and rammed earth.

**Significance:**
Designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1987, the Great Wall of China is not only a symbol of the Chinese nation but also exemplifies the profound historical significance and architectural grandeur. It represents one of the most impressive architectural feats in human history.

**Myths and Visibility:**
Contrary to popular belief, the Great Wall of China is not visible from space with the naked eye due to its narrowness and the fact that it follows the natural contours of the landscape, making it less discernible.

**Cultural Impact:**
The Great Wall is a national symbol of China, embodying its strength, resilience, and a long history of cultural significance. It continues to be an invaluable cultural treasure and one of the country's most popular tourist destinations, offering insight into China's ancient history. 

**Preservation:**
Sections such as those at Badaling and Mutianyu are well-restored and popular among visitors. Efforts continue to be made to preserve this monumental structure, which remains a testament to human ingenuity and endurance.
