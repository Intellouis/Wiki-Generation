**Great Wall of China**

*Also Known As:* Wan-li Changcheng

*Category:* Geography & Travel

*Chinese Pinyin:* Wanli Changcheng

*Wade-Giles Romanization:* Wan-li Ch'ang-ch'eng

**Overview:**
The Great Wall of China, an ancient series of walls and fortifications located in eastern Asia, represents one of humanity's most extraordinary feats of engineering. Originally established by various warring states, the wall was unified by Qin Shi Huang, the first Emperor of China, around 220 B.C.E. Over successive dynasties, the structure was expanded and reinforced, peaking during the Ming dynasty (1368â€“1644). Recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1987, it symbolizes not only China's historical resilience and architectural prowess but also serves as an iconic cultural monument.

**Construction History:**
Construction of the Great Wall began independently by several Chinese states from as early as the 7th century B.C.E. to safeguard against northern nomadic tribes. Throughout its history, the wall was extended and rebuilt by various dynasties, most notably the Ming, who are responsible for much of the most well-preserved sections today.

**Geographic Span:**
The Great Wall stretches from Shanhaiguan in Hebei province to Jiayuguan in Gansu province. Extensive research and recent surveys have found that the full scope of the Great Wall, with all its branches, extends to a staggering 13,170 miles. The most famous section, built during the Ming dynasty, spans approximately 5,500 miles.

**Materials and Design:**
The wall's construction includes materials ranging from earth and wood to bricks and stone, depending on the region and availability. It follows the natural terrain of mountains and rivers, often utilizing these features as defensive barriers. The architecture features watchtowers, barracks, garrison stations, and signaling capabilities through smoke or fire.

**Cultural Significance:**
Beyond its primary military defensive role, the Great Wall has become an emblem of the Chinese nation, shaping its identity and contributing to its mythology and lore. It attracts countless visitors each year who come to witness its grandeur and the natural beauty of its surroundings.

**Visibility from Space:**
Contrary to popular belief, the Great Wall of China is not visible from space with the naked eye. The myth persists due to its immense scale, but from low Earth orbit, it appears to blend in with the surrounding natural landscape.

**Preservation Efforts:**
Despite some sections being in disrepair, conservation initiatives aim to protect and restore this symbol of Chinese heritage. The continuing efforts ensure that the integrity and significance of the Great Wall are maintained for future generations.

**Interesting Facts:**
- The wall's fortifications include turrets, watchtowers, and troop barracks.
- Over the millennia, countless laborers, including soldiers, peasants, and prisoners, were enlisted to build the wall, often under harsh conditions.
- The wall plays a significant role in Chinese folklore and art, symbolizing the boundary between civilization and barbarism.

**Further Research:**
Visitors and scholars continue to explore the Great Wall, revealing new aspects of its history and construction. Ongoing archaeological efforts contribute to the understanding of its full expanse, complexity, and cultural meaning.
